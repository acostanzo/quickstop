# ADR 008 — The lint-posture rubric is the authority; lintguini's templates are mechanical projections

## Context

Lintguini is expanding from a Pronto-sibling auditor into a full lint toolkit — `/lintguini:configure`, `/lintguini:lint`, `/lintguini:format`, `/lintguini:fix` — alongside the existing `/lintguini:audit`. The expansion introduces per-language config templates (`templates/python/`, `templates/javascript/`, …) that `/lintguini:configure` writes into consumer repos, plus strictness flags (`--strict | --lenient | --minimal`) that pick a band within those templates.

A canonical definition of "good lint posture" already exists in pronto's reference set at `plugins/pronto/references/roll-your-own/lint-posture.md`. The rubric covers the same languages lintguini's templates target — Python (ruff), JavaScript / TypeScript (biome or eslint+prettier), Rust (rustfmt + clippy), Ruby (rubocop or standardrb), Go (golangci-lint + gofmt) — and is the document pronto's audit grades against. It is the definition of "what good looks like" that the wider quickstop ecosystem reads.

The question is *where* per-language strictness baselines live, and *what the contract is* between the rubric and lintguini's templates. Two natural homes exist: lintguini owns its own per-language strictness definitions (templates and rubric coexist as sibling documents that may agree or drift), or the rubric is the authority and lintguini's templates are mechanical projections of it (the templates have no independent existence). The decision constrains how strictness flags map to baselines, what happens when the rubric updates, who can extend strictness levels or add languages, and how drift between the two is treated. It must be settled before M1 lands the templates, since the templates' shape and provenance depend on it.

The constraint that frames the choice: pronto's audit already grades repos against the rubric. If lintguini configures a repo to a strictness band that does not match the rubric, pronto's audit will fail the repo lintguini just configured — a contradictory user experience the toolkit must not produce.

## Decision

**The lint-posture rubric is the authority. Lintguini's templates are mechanical projections of the rubric, not parallel definitions.** When the rubric and a template disagree, the rubric wins; the template is the bug. Lintguini's `--strict | --lenient | --minimal` flags are bounded variations *within* the rubric's per-language bands, never extensions of it.

This imposes the following rules:

1. **Source of truth is `plugins/pronto/references/roll-your-own/lint-posture.md`.** Every template under `plugins/lintguini/templates/<lang>/` derives its rule set, version pinning, and file shape from the corresponding section in the rubric.
2. **New strictness levels land in the rubric first.** If a future strictness band (e.g., `--paranoid`) is wanted, it lands as a rubric update first; lintguini's templates follow. A strictness flag without a rubric-defined band is a violation.
3. **New languages land in the rubric first.** Adding a language to lintguini without a corresponding rubric section is a violation. The rubric defines what good looks like for the language; lintguini implements that definition.
4. **Generated configs are self-describing.** The first non-blank line of every config lintguini writes is a comment naming the rubric reference: `# Generated by lintguini — see plugins/pronto/references/roll-your-own/lint-posture.md for the source of truth.` A future author touching the file must know where to look.
5. **The audit grades against the rubric, not against templates.** When `score-linter-presence.sh` and `score-formatter-presence.sh` evaluate a configured repo, they compare against the rubric's per-language baselines. A repo configured by `/lintguini:configure --strict` must score at the rubric's strict band — that round-trip is acceptance bar A1 of the expansion plan.

The rubric's coverage today is Python, JavaScript / TypeScript, Rust, Ruby, and Go. Lintguini's M1 templates cover exactly that set. Anything beyond is out of scope for this plan.

## Consequences

### Positive

- **Single source of truth.** Authors looking up "what's the strict baseline for Ruby?" go to the rubric. There is no second document that might disagree. The toolkit's behaviour is predictable from a document users already read.
- **Audit-and-configure consistency.** A repo configured by `/lintguini:configure --strict` will score at the rubric's strict band on `/lintguini:audit`, by construction. The contradiction-failure mode where lintguini configures a repo pronto then fails is structurally impossible.
- **Rubric updates flow downhill.** When the rubric tightens a baseline (e.g., adding a new ruff rule to the strict set), the change to lintguini's templates is mechanical — an author projects the rubric's diff into the matching template. No re-litigation, no second decision.
- **No author burden across two documents.** Authors maintain the rubric. Templates are derived. There is no second registry to keep current.

### Negative

- **Lintguini cannot ship a strictness innovation without a rubric change.** If a maintainer wants to experiment with a new band or language in lintguini, they must update the rubric first. This is friction by design, but it is friction.
- **Templates are derivative, not independent.** Reviewers reading a template diff in isolation cannot tell whether the diff is rubric-faithful without consulting the rubric. The provenance is real but not visually local.
- **Cross-repo coordination cost.** A rubric change at `plugins/pronto/references/roll-your-own/lint-posture.md` and the corresponding template change at `plugins/lintguini/templates/<lang>/` are atomic in intent but may land in two commits or two PRs; reviewers need to verify the projection is faithful.

### Neutral

- **The contract is one-way.** The rubric does not depend on lintguini; lintguini depends on the rubric. Pronto can ship rubric updates without coordinating with lintguini's release cadence — lintguini catches up at its own pace, and the audit tells consumers when their config has fallen behind.
- **Drift becomes a detectable bug class.** If a template diverges from the rubric, pronto's audit on a lintguini-configured repo will surface the gap as a presence-but-not-strict observation. This is the intended detection mechanism; it doesn't need a separate drift-checker tool.
- **Conditional audit scorers grade against the rubric.** The M5 `score-lint-pass-rate.sh` runs the configured linter and grades pass rate against rubric expectations, not against lintguini-internal definitions.

## Alternatives considered

### Lintguini owns its own per-language strictness definitions

Have `plugins/lintguini/templates/<lang>/` carry independent strictness definitions, with the rubric and the templates as sibling documents that may or may not agree. Differences between them are legitimate variation — the rubric describes one community's view, the toolkit describes another.

Rejected. Pronto's audit grades against the rubric. If lintguini configures a repo to a strictness band that doesn't match the rubric, pronto's audit on that same repo will fail it — `/lintguini:configure --strict` followed by `/pronto:audit` would produce a failure on the dimension lintguini just configured. That is a contradictory user experience the toolkit must not produce. The deeper problem: two documents purporting to define the same thing inevitably drift, and the cost of reconciling them at every audit run (or worse, leaving them silently divergent) outweighs the freedom of independent definition.

### Embed the rubric inside lintguini

Move `plugins/pronto/references/roll-your-own/lint-posture.md` into lintguini and have pronto reference lintguini's copy. Lintguini becomes the single source for both the templates and the rubric.

Rejected. The rubric is consumed by pronto's "roll-your-own" reference set — it is the document a consumer reads to set up lint-posture *without* installing lintguini. Moving it into lintguini hides the canonical definition behind a plugin that the consumer hasn't installed yet, defeating the rubric's purpose. The rubric must live where pronto's audit and pronto's "no plugin needed" guidance can both reach it; that's `plugins/pronto/references/roll-your-own/`.

### Author-maintained per-language registries inside lintguini

Have a `plugins/lintguini/strictness.json` that lists rules per language per band, with templates rendered from it at install time. Lintguini owns the registry; the rubric is descriptive prose that documents the registry.

Rejected. This inverts the authority — the registry becomes the source of truth and the rubric becomes documentation of the registry. The same drift problem reappears (the prose rubric and the JSON registry can disagree), with the additional cost of forcing authors to maintain a JSON shape lintguini invented, when each ecosystem already has its own conventional config shape (`pyproject.toml`, `biome.json`, etc.). The conventional shapes are themselves the registry; the rubric describes which keys/values to set; lintguini writes them.
